Understanding Disease

This page helps you identify common diseases, understand their signs, and learn effective prevention strategies. We've included specific advice for farmers to protect their crops and livestock, especially important for the agricultural backbone of Uganda.

Infectious Diseases

Caused by pathogens like bacteria, viruses, fungi, or parasites. They can spread from person to person, animal to animal, or through the environment.

Non-Infectious Diseases

Caused by environmental factors (e.g., pollution), nutritional deficiencies, lifestyle choices, or genetics. These are not typically contagious.

Common Human Diseases & Symptoms

Knowing the signs can lead to early diagnosis and better outcomes. Here are some prevalent health concerns in Uganda.

Malaria

**Symptoms:** Fever, chills, headache, muscle aches, fatigue, nausea, vomiting, diarrhea.

**Prevention:** Use treated mosquito nets, indoor residual spraying, repellent, prompt diagnosis and treatment.

Typhoid Fever

**Symptoms:** Prolonged fever, fatigue, headache, nausea, abdominal pain, constipation or diarrhea.

**Prevention:** Drink safe water, practice good hygiene (handwashing), proper sanitation, cook food thoroughly.

HIV/AIDS

**Symptoms:** Flu-like symptoms initially, then can be asymptomatic for years. Later stages involve weight loss, chronic diarrhea, opportunistic infections.

**Prevention:** Safe sexual practices, use of condoms, pre-exposure prophylaxis (PrEP), avoiding needle sharing.

Tuberculosis (TB)

**Symptoms:** Persistent cough (2+ weeks), chest pain, fever, night sweats, weight loss, fatigue.

**Prevention:** Early diagnosis and treatment, BCG vaccine (for children), good ventilation, covering coughs/sneezes.

Childhood Immunizable Diseases

**Examples:** Measles, Polio, Diphtheria, Whooping Cough, Tetanus.

**Prevention:** Ensure children receive all routine vaccinations as per the national immunization schedule.

Protecting Your Farm: Crop & Livestock Health

For Ugandan farmers, healthy crops and livestock mean healthy livelihoods. Learn to spot and prevent common agricultural diseases.

Cassava Brown Streak Disease (CBSD)

**Signs:** Yellowing along leaf veins, brown streaks on stems, root rot and necrosis rendering tubers inedible.

**Prevention:** Use disease-free planting material, practice crop rotation, remove and destroy infected plants, control whiteflies.

Coffee Wilt Disease (CWD)

**Signs:** Sudden wilting of leaves, rapid defoliation, blackening of stems from base upwards, eventual death of the tree.

**Prevention:** Plant resistant varieties, prune correctly, use clean tools, uproot and burn infected trees immediately.

African Swine Fever (ASF)

**Signs:** High fever, loss of appetite, skin reddening, bleeding from nose/rectum, uncoordinated movements, high mortality.

**Prevention:** Strict biosecurity measures, restrict movement of pigs/pork, no current vaccine, quarantine new animals.

Foot-and-Mouth Disease (FMD)

**Signs:** Blisters on tongue, lips, gums, nostrils, teats, and between hooves; lameness, excessive salivation, reduced milk yield.

**Prevention:** Vaccination, quarantine infected animals, disinfection of premises and equipment, control animal movement.

Empowering Prevention Strategies

Prevention is always better than cure. Simple practices can make a huge difference in protecting both human and agricultural health.

Hygiene & Sanitation

Regular handwashing with soap and water, safe disposal of waste, clean water sources, and proper latrine use are fundamental.

Vaccination & Immunization

Ensure all recommended human vaccinations are up-to-date. For livestock, follow veterinary vaccination schedules diligently.

Food Safety & Nutrition

Proper food handling, cooking, and storage. A balanced diet strengthens immunity for both humans and animals.

Vector Control

Control mosquitoes (nets, repellents), flies, rodents, and other disease-carrying vectors in homes and on farms.

Farm Biosecurity

Strict measures to prevent disease entry and spread on farms: controlled access, disinfection, quarantine for new animals.

Early Detection & Reporting

Be vigilant for symptoms. Report suspected human or animal diseases to local health workers or veterinary officers promptly.
